Let’s start with a common narrative: Like other technological revolutions, AI first replaces some labor, then creates new jobs, and ultimately boosts productivity across society.

This statement is not wrong, but it misses the most critical element.

There is a fundamental difference between AI and previous revolutions such as steam power, electricity, and information technology. Steam engines replaced muscle power; electricity transmitted energy; computers replaced structured mental labor. Their core targets of substitution were tasks that could already be clearly defined and standardized. What AI is truly tackling this time is something previously thought to be difficult for machines to master: tacit knowledge.

What is tacit knowledge? 

It refers to skills you perform well but cannot easily articulate. You can judge whether a plan is reliable, yet you cannot pinpoint exactly which details led to that judgment. You can sense what a client is truly worried about, but those signals are embedded in tone and context, making them hard to codify into Standard Operating Procedures (SOPs). You can navigate complex negotiations with a difficult partner, but someone else might get stuck.

![](https://pbs.twimg.com/media/HJZ0kqHbgAE_BAc.jpg)

These elements used to form the moat for knowledge workers. Because they are uncodable, they are unreplicable. Because they are unreplicable, they possess scarcity and bargaining power.

The key distinction of AI lies in its ability not just to process encoded knowledge, but to make a portion of tacit knowledge encodable.

The economic implications of this go far deeper than simply “replacing mental labor.”

Most traditional technological revolutions first disrupted physical labor, allowing workers to transition into mental work through education and training. The late 20th-century information technology revolution began impacting certain structured mental tasks—such as calculation, archiving, and basic accounting—but still left a substantial buffer zone: capabilities that are “hard to describe but easy to execute.” These abilities rely on experience, intuition, and interpersonal judgment, are difficult to program, and have long been considered a human preserve.

However, large language models and multimodal models are changing this premise.

Their core capability does not lie in executing pre-written rules. Instead, by training on massive datasets, they learn patterns. Many of these patterns are ones humans themselves cannot fully articulate, yet they undeniably exist. Consequently, a portion of the judgment previously exclusive to senior employees is becoming extractable, replicable, and scalable.

![](https://pbs.twimg.com/media/HJZ1u1JaMAAlaxT.jpg)

What does this mean?

Consider a top structural engineer in a company who can spot potential issues at a glance after reviewing a few drawings. This was once purely experience-based. Now, if a model can achieve similar results, this knowledge shifts from an unreplicable personal asset to a replicable organizational asset.

Take another example. A veteran salesperson’s ability to assess clients was traditionally passed down slowly through mentorship. If a model can learn similar patterns from call records, email correspondence, and contextual data, then this tacit knowledge becomes partially encoded. Once encoded, it no longer depends on a specific individual but can be captured, optimized, and replicated by the organization.

This is a crucial entry point for understanding the economic essence of AI.

AI is not about making people work harder or machines run faster; it is about altering the boundaries of knowledge replicability.

Who benefits?

The first tier of beneficiaries are organizations capable of沉淀 (consolidating) tacit knowledge and transforming it into systemic capabilities. They become less dependent on star individuals, converting high-value judgment into scalable services.

The second tier comprises those adept at leveraging these systems. An individual with strong judgment, paired with an AI that has absorbed the organization’s tacit knowledge, may outperform a mediocre team from the past.

Who risks marginalization?

Mid-level knowledge workers. Previously, they could command premiums based on their tacit knowledge. As this knowledge becomes encoded and automated, and if they lack higher-order judgment skills to migrate upward, they face a precarious position. They are neither top-tier experts nor the cheapest executors, but rather the “experienced but no longer scarce” middle layer susceptible to system replacement.

![](https://pbs.twimg.com/media/HJZ2YIma4AAHxOx.jpg)

Another divergence from past technological revolutions is worth noting. Previous shocks often started with low-skilled labor and moved upward. In contrast, this wave of AI disruption may move in reverse: it initially targets knowledge work requiring certain judgment capabilities but not necessarily top-tier creativity.

Of course, a sobering caveat is necessary.

The encodability of tacit knowledge remains highly localized at present. Models excel in areas with clear patterns, but they are still far behind in domains characterized by extreme uncertainty, cross-domain analogy, and decision-making under severe information incompleteness. Current breakthroughs are point-specific, not comprehensive replacements.

Therefore, within an economic framework, AI’s essence is not a new factor of production, but a mechanism that alters how knowledge is distributed. It drastically reduces the replication cost of certain types of knowledge while relatively increasing the scarcity of others.

This will not be a gentle transition. The identity, career paths, and organizational structures of knowledge workers are largely built upon the unreplicability of knowledge. As this premise begins to loosen, many traditional definitions regarding profession, experience, and value will need rewriting.

Whether the future holds a more equal or a more polarized knowledge economy does not depend solely on the strength of the models, but on how organizations and society distribute the gains and costs of this technological shift. Past technological revolutions have provided ample warnings on this front.
